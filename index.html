Here‚Äôs a short post (for myself, I don‚Äôt expect anyone else to read it) about Ruth Millikan‚Äôs concept of proper function. Very roughly: A proper function of something is a function performed by its ancestors that helps explain why that thing exists. So a proper function of a heart is to pump blood. But it seems to me that given the ‚Äúprecariousness of existence‚Äù of organisms, the proper function of an individual body part or behavior is pretty insignificant!

In Language, Thought and other Biological Categories Millikan defines a proper function as follows. The definition uses terms she‚Äôs defined previously, but you can get the gist of it even without seeing the previous definitions.

Where m is a member of a reproductively established family R and R has the reproductively established or Normal character C, m has the function F as a direct proper function iff:

(1) Certain ancestors of m performed F.

(2) In part because there existed a direct causal connection between having the character C and performance of the function F in the case of these ancestors of m, C correlated positively with F over a certain set of items S which included here ancestors and other things not having C.

(3) One among the legitimate explanations that can be given of the fact that m exists makes reference to the fact that C correlated positively with F over S, either directly causing reproduction or m or explaining why R was proliferated and hence why m exists.

In the next paragraph she says that hearts have direct proper functions, provided our guesses about their evolution are correct. Here m would be the heart in a given organism, R I guess would be the family of hearts in the organism‚Äôs lineage, F might be pumping blood, and then C would be the reproducible (genetic) properties of members of R that cause (some of) them to pump blood.

The problem I think is in (3). This is where the ‚Äúprecariousness of existence‚Äù comes in, as discussed by Derek Parfit and others. A given organism conceived of two gametes is essentially the organism conceived of those very two gametes. Different sperm and egg, different organism. Thus an organism‚Äôs existence is ‚Äúprecarious‚Äù: if its conception had occurred at any other moment, it would probably have involved a different pair of gametes, hence that individual organism would never have existed.

So if your distant ancestor‚Äôs heart had never pumped blood, you wouldn‚Äôt exist, so your heart wouldn‚Äôt exist either. So far, so good. But what if that distant, ocean-dwelling ancestor‚Äôs heart had pumped blood better than it actually did, enough to measurably change that ancestor‚Äôs life? Also then, you and your heart very likely wouldn‚Äôt exist!

Now consider Millikan‚Äôs condition (3) and the correlation of C with F. Yes, the fact that it‚Äôs positive explains ‚Äúwhy R was proliferated and hence why m exists.‚Äù If your ancestors‚Äô hearts had pumped blood less effectively than their evolutionary competitors‚Äô, R might not have proliferated. But if the correlation were greater than it actually was, R would have proliferated differently and again, because of the ‚Äúprecariousness of existence‚Äù, you and your heart would not exist.

In other words, it‚Äôs not only the fact that (roughly speaking) C has caused F per se that explains m‚Äôs existence. That‚Äôs a true partial explanation but a misleading one. It‚Äôs rather the fact that C correlated with F exactly as it actually did, not worse and not better either! So the explanation in condition (3) seems true but insignificantly so, because of the precariousness of m‚Äôs existence.

This is well said by Chantal Mouffe on the ‚Äúgood democrats‚Äù who substitute moralizing for politics in their response to what they call the ‚Äúextreme right‚Äù. Published in 2005, obviously drawing on Carl Schmitt, still very relevant today.

As we saw earlier, the dominant discourse announces the end of the adversarial model of politics and the advent of a consensual politics beyond left and right. However, politics always entails an us/ them distinction. This is why the consensus advocated by the defenders of the ‚Äònon-partisan democracy‚Äô cannot exist without drawing a frontier and defining an exterior, a ‘them’ which assures the identity of the consensus and secures the coherence of the ‚Äòus'. To put it in another way, the consensus at the centre, which is supposed to include everybody in our post-political societies, cannot exist without the establishment of a frontier, because no consensus - or no common identity, for that matter - can exist without a frontier. There cannot be an ‘us’ without a ‘them’, and the very identity of a group depends on the existence of a ‘constitutive outside‚Äô. So the us of the ‚Äògood democrats’ needs to be secured by the determination of a ‘them’. Nowadays the ‚Äòthem' is provided by what is designated as the ‘extreme right‚Äô. This term is used in a very undefined way to refer to an amalgam of groups and parties whose characteristics and objectives are extremely diverse and it covers a wide spectrum which goes from fringe groups of extremists, skinheads and neo-nazis to the authoritarian right and a variety of right-wing populist parties.

Such a heterogeneous construct is of course useless to grasp the nature and causes of the new forms of right-wing politics. But it is very useful to secure the identity of the ‘good democrats‚Äô and to procure a positive image of the post-political consensus. It is clear that, since politics has supposedly become ‘non-adversarial’, the ‘them’ which is necessary to make possible the ‘us’ of the good democrats cannot be envisaged as a political adversary, and the frontier has to be drawn in the moral register. So, to draw the frontier between the ‘good democrats and the ‘evil extreme-right’ is very convenient, since the them’ can now be considered as a sort of moral disease which needs to be condemned morally, not fought politically. This is why no attempt is made to try to understand the reasons for its existence - an understanding, in any case, made impossible by the amalgam on which the very notion of ‘extreme-right’ is based. Moreover, attempts at understanding are deemed suspect, and perceived as a move towards condoning something which is morally unacceptable. As a consequence, moral condemnation and the establishment of a cordon sanitaire have become the dominant answers to the rise of right-wing populist movements.

The increasing moralisation of political discourse that we are witnessing goes hand in hand with the dominant post-political perspective. Far from indicating a new stage in the triumphant march of democracy, such a phenomenon represents a very negative development. Let’s not misunderstand my point. It is not my intention to defend Realpolitik and to deny that normative concerns should play a role in politics. But there is a big difference between morality and moralism, which limits itself to the denunciation of evil in others. Yet today’s good democrats are so confident that they have the truth, and that their mission is to impose it on others, that they refuse to engage in debate with those who disagree.

It is no doubt easier to present them as a moral enemy, to be destroyed and eradicated, instead of having to envisage them as adversaries in the political terrain.
